What changed — Today View
Before
Thick 8px progress bars in pure green / yellow / red feel like a traffic light — a generic dashboard widget, not a health app with any opinion.
After
A 2px hairline track with a single amber accent ring communicates the same data with far less visual noise. Macros use muted, harmonious tones.
Before
The fire emoji 🔥 and a bold "753" on a blue card is the single most common pattern in calorie apps. It reads as a template.
After
The calorie ring is contained and quiet. The number lives inside a progress arc — meaning is encoded, not just labeled. Stat column beside it earns its space.
Before
Bold white on cold dark blue cards feel lifted from a fitness tracking Figma kit. Zero warmth, zero personality.
After
Warm near-black (#0F0D0A) and off-white (#F5F0E6) create a matte, editorial feel. The background itself has temperature.
Before
The floating blue rounded-square "+" tab is a familiar cliché that disrupts the tab bar rhythm and draws attention away from content.
After
Flat, equal-weight tab items with consistent iconography. The + action moves to a small persistent FAB when actually needed — not permanently dominating the nav.

Core principles applied
Warm palette over cold blue
#0F0D0A background, #F5F0E6 text, #C4965A amber accent. Warm tones read as intentional; cold dark-navy reads as default.
Weight through whitespace, not borders
Cards are gone. Content groups are separated by 1px hairlines and spacing, not thick rounded-rect cards that tile the whole screen.
One accent, used sparingly
Amber appears only where it means something: the progress ring, the active tab, the add button. Not sprayed across every interactive element.
Typography does the work
Inter Light at large sizes for data, regular for content, a strict 3-level hierarchy. No bold-everything, no all-caps on every element.
Data viz that earns its shape
The calorie ring encodes progress in its arc. The week bars show trend at a glance. The meal distribution is a single linear bar — not a donut that requires a legend to decode.
Menu as a document, not a form
Items are plain rows with hairline separators, grouped by station. The add button is small and inline, not a big badge. You're browsing food, not filling out a spreadsheet.
